Basic Sentence Structure & Verb Conjugation

How German verbs change based on who is doing the action

The Core Concept: Why Verbs Change

Just like in Urdu — Main peeta hoon / Tu peeta hai / Woh peeta hai — German verbs change their ending depending on the subject. The verb "stem" stays the same; only the ending changes.

VERB = STEM + ENDING
e.g. lernen → stem: lern- + ending: e/st/t/en

📐 The E-ST-T-EN Rule (Present Tense Endings)

EnglishUrduGerman PronounEndinglernenspielenarbeiten
IMainich-elernespielearbeite
You (informal)Tudu-stlernstspielstarbeitest
He/She/ItWoher/sie/es-tlerntspieltarbeitet
WeHumwir-enlernenspielenarbeiten
You (plural)Tumihr-tlerntspieltarbeitet
They / You (formal)Woh / Aapsie / Sie-enlernenspielenarbeiten
💡 Memory Tip

ich and er/sie/es often rhyme! ich lerne → er lernt. Also: wir and sie/Sie always share the same ending (-en = the full dictionary form).

Standard Sentence Structure: Subject → Verb → Object

In German, the verb is always the second element in a standard sentence. This is called V2 word order.

Ich trinke Tee.
I drink tea.
Main chai peeta hoon.
Wir lernen Deutsch.
We learn German.
Hum German seekhte hain.
Er spielt Fußball.
He plays football.
Woh football khelta hai.
Sie kocht Reis.
She cooks rice.
Woh chawal pakati hai.
Du kaufst Brot.
You buy bread.
Tu roti khareedta hai.
Ihr macht Hausaufgaben.
You all do homework.
Tum homework karte ho.
Es regnet heute.
It rains today.
Aaj barish ho rahi hai.
Wir essen Pizza.
We eat pizza.
Hum pizza khate hain.
Sie schlafen früh.
They sleep early.
Woh jaldi sote hain.
⚠ When the sentence starts with something other than the subject

If you begin with a time/place word, the subject and verb swap — verb stays in position 2!


Heute trinkt er Kaffee. → Today he drinks coffee. (Subject "er" moves after verb)

Morgen arbeite ich. → Tomorrow I work.

The 4 Special Consonant Rules

🔤 Rule 1: The "CH" Rule

CH has TWO sounds depending on the vowel before it:

CH
after i, e, ä, ö, ü →
soft "sh/hy"
ich · Milch · Küche · mich · möchten
CH
after a, o, u, au →
hard "Kh"
machen · Buch · kochen · auch · Dach

🔤 Rule 2: The "-IG" Ending

When a word ends in -ig, it sounds like "ish" (soft ch):

Introductions & "Sein" (To Be)

The most important irregular verb — hona in German

Just like English "I am / you are / he is" and Urdu "main hoon / tu hai / woh hai" — sein doesn't follow any pattern. You must memorize it!

Modal Verbs & W-Questions

Helper verbs expressing ability, obligation, desire — and asking questions

The Modal Verb Sentence Structure (Critical!)

Subject → Modal Verb (position 2) → ... → Main Verb (END, in -en form)
The modal is conjugated. The main verb stays in its dictionary (-en) form at the very end.
Ich kann Tennis spielen.
I can play tennis.
Main tennis khel sakta hoon.
Wir wollen Pizza essen.
We want to eat pizza.
Hum pizza khana chahte hain.
Er muss heute arbeiten.
He must work today.
Usse aaj kaam karna hai.
Sie möchte Wasser trinken.
She would like to drink water.
Woh paani peena chahti hai.
Du darfst hier nicht rauchen.
You may not smoke here.
Yahan dhoomrapan mana hai.
Ich soll mehr lernen.
I should learn more.
Mujhe zyada padhna chahiye.

W-Questions (W-Fragen)

💡 No "do" in German!

Unlike English, German does NOT use "do/does" for questions. The question word comes first, then the verb — just like Urdu: "Aap kahan rehte hain?" (Where you live?)

Yes/No Questions & Polite Requests

Invert the verb — and master the golden request formula

Yes/No Questions: Move the Verb to Position 1

Verb → Subject → Rest → ?
Statement: Sie spielen Tennis. → Question: Spielen Sie Tennis?
